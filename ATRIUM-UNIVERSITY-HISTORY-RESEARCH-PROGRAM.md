# Atrium University History Research Program

**Status:** Enacted research and acquisition policy  
**Evidence snapshot:** 2026-07-20  
**Applies to:** Atrium history discovery records, point launches, journeys, and future history packs  
**Does not authorize:** publication, excerpt packaging, translation, or specialist sign-off

## 1. Executive decision

Atrium will use leading university history programs as a **comparative research instrument**, not as a canon and not as a source of runtime text. Rankings help identify a broad cohort; official program requirements, public syllabi, and course materials reveal durable pedagogical questions. Atrium's own editorial constitution, primary evidence, specialist review, and edition-level rights audit remain authoritative.

The revised program privileges subjects with high causal and connective power:

1. political institutions, constitutions, law, and administration;
2. war, diplomacy, strategy, and state formation;
3. taxation, public debt, trade, finance, and industrialization;
4. political thought, intellectual history, and historiography;
5. religion, theology, toleration, and secularization;
6. science, technology, infrastructure, and material culture;
7. cities, agriculture, population, migration, and disease;
8. empire as governance, commerce, military power, and diplomacy;
9. biography, decision, contingency, and historical agency;
10. archives, source criticism, disputed evidence, and the limits of knowledge.

This is an anti-presentist orientation, not an exclusion policy. Race, sex, slavery, class, and other social categories enter when the historical actors, institutions, law, evidence, or causal problem require them. No category receives a representational quota, and no indispensable cause is suppressed to satisfy a preference for less-politicized material.

## 2. Research question

The program asks:

> Which durable questions, methods, and source practices recur across excellent history programs, and which of them expose consequential weaknesses in the present Atrium corpus?

It does **not** ask which university supplies the correct interpretation. A syllabus records a designed course, not a scholarly consensus. A ranking records a measurement system, not historical truth.

## 3. Evidence hierarchy

| Tier | Evidence | Permitted use | Prohibited inference |
|---|---|---|---|
| U1 | Current official program requirements and handbooks | Identify durable requirements: breadth, methods, research, chronological and geographical range | Treat the institution's distribution categories as Atrium quotas |
| U2 | Official public syllabi, lecture schedules, and course descriptions | Identify recurring problems, useful comparisons, and historiographic fault lines | Copy readings, lectures, annotations, or modern prose into runtime |
| U3 | Official specialist programs and methods courses | Stress-test neglected causal dimensions | Let one specialist field displace general historical balance |
| U4 | University-hosted archives and digital collections | Discover primary sources and stable citations | Infer that hosting grants reuse rights |
| U5 | Rankings and reputation surveys | Cohort discovery only | Score content or settle an interpretation |

Every source is rechecked at the beginning of a content tranche because curricula, course availability, URLs, and licensing can change.

## 5. What the cohort actually teaches Atrium

Across otherwise different programs, six structural commitments recur:

1. **Breadth and concentration coexist.** Atrium should keep a bounded question while connecting it across time, geography, and field.
2. **Method is explicit.** A beautiful timeline is not enough; the product must disclose what kind of source supports a claim and where inference begins.
3. **Primary sources are interpreted, not displayed as self-explanatory relics.** Each launch needs a historical problem.
4. **Historiography is plural.** Competing causal explanations should be recorded without turning the runtime into a survey of modern opinions.
5. **Research culminates in synthesis.** Journeys should make a defensible argument or stage a real conflict, not concatenate excerpts.
6. **Chronological grounding matters.** Revolutionary ideas must be connected to inherited institutions, wars, debts, religious structures, and older political languages.

These findings are an inference from the official program evidence above. They are not claims that every course or historian shares one interpretation.

## 6. Atrium research record contract

Before a new historical event, point launch, or journey enters acquisition, its research record must contain:

| Field | Requirement |
|---|---|
| `researchQuestion` | One bounded causal or interpretive question |
| `universitySignals` | At least two independent U1/U2 signals, or one U1/U2 signal plus a documented primary-source imperative |
| `historiographicProblem` | Competing explanations or a statement that no material dispute was found |
| `primarySourceCandidates` | Exact documents, creators, dates, archive/edition candidates, and proposed locators |
| `sourceAsymmetries` | Missing, mediated, translated, state-produced, retrospective, or otherwise biased evidence |
| `counterEvidence` | The strongest source or fact that complicates the proposed framing |
| `coverageTargets` | Existing Atrium records and journeys repaired or connected |
| `launchForm` | Point, journey, contextual-only event, or method lens |
| `runtimeThesis` | The experience's provisional argument in one sentence |
| `rightsPosture` | Per-edition status; never provider-wide |
| `reviewState` | Research, source audit, rights audit, editorial review, specialist review, runtime review |
| `reviewedAt` | Date and accountable reviewer identity |

University reading lists may guide bibliography and historiography. Modern scholarship remains commentary or link-only unless separately licensed; it is never silently converted into Chamber payload.

## 7. Content selection score

Candidates receive 0–4 points on each positive dimension:

- **causal centrality** — how necessary the subject is to explaining the period;
- **coverage repair** — how directly it clears a blocked journey or unlaunchable record;
- **primary-source feasibility** — quality, authenticity, locator stability, and viable edition path;
- **connective power** — number and importance of genealogical or chronological links;
- **interpretive plurality** — capacity to sustain a real comparison or dispute;
- **experience fitness** — ability to form a coherent 3–7-minute point or 8–18-minute journey.

Then subtract:

- 0–4 for **rights fragility**;
- 0–3 for **redundancy** with existing launches;
- 0–4 for **presentist dependence**, meaning the proposal makes sense chiefly through current political categories rather than actors, institutions, evidence, and historically situated questions.

| Final score | Action |
|---|---|
| 19–24 | Tier A: acquire next |
| 15–18 | Tier B: research and stage after Tier A |
| 11–14 | Tier C: retain as a documented candidate |
| 10 or below | Reject or fundamentally reframe |

A high score opens research; it does not bypass source, rights, editorial, specialist, or runtime gates.

5. **Atlantic-order packet — completed in 1.10:** the 1783 treaty text and paired French/Haitian 1825 recognition records join cleared Paris 1763, Jamaica Letter, and Monroe material; no extra treaty was added merely to inflate coverage.
6. **Industrial-systems packet — completed in 1.11:** Watt's official patent reprint, Baines's scan-backed Arkwright discussion and embedded specification, Marsden's power-loom development chapter, and the identified Stockton–Darlington company notice and opening report create four independent point launches. The runtime excludes modern catalogue prose and does not treat a patent date as proof of a mature machine.
7. **War-for-Independence packet — completed in 1.12:** scan-backed Continental Congress evidence is paired with Gage's report rather than synthesized into a false neutral voice; Washington's orders, the signed Franco-American alliance, Yorktown's capitulation, and the existing 1783 treaty form the institutional spine. The journey does not claim comprehensive campaign coverage.

No packet may use a modern course reading as substitute runtime content.

## 10. Journey design standard

Every historical journey must have:

- one question that can be understood before launch;
- a chronological or causal spine;
- at least two historically situated positions when the subject is contested;
- no repeated passage, padding, or synthetic primary voice;
- transitions that distinguish event, document, retrospective account, and editorial context;
- a conclusion that clarifies what changed, what endured, and what remains disputed;
- 8–18 minutes of genuine, reviewed material under the canonical compiler;
- at least one independently useful 3–7-minute point launch when the source supports it.

“Multiple perspectives” means perspectives necessary to the historical problem. It is not a demographic checklist and cannot be satisfied by token excerpt insertion.

## 11. Research and publication gates

| Gate | Pass condition |
|---|---|
| R0 — question | Bounded question, score, corpus repair target, and non-goals recorded |
| R1 — university triangulation | Required U1/U2 signals logged; disagreements retained |
| R2 — historiography | Major causal interpretations and strongest counterargument summarized from lawful access; modern text remains link-only by default |
| R3 — source inventory | Primary candidates, archives, editions, locators, languages, and source asymmetries recorded |
| R4 — rights | Exact payload edition/translation cleared for the target jurisdiction; exclusions logged |
| R5 — editorial | Transcript/collation, dates, names, context, and claims independently reviewed |
| R6 — specialist | Relevant specialist approves the claim set and interpretive balance with a version-bound attestation |
| R7 — experience | Point/journey durations, transitions, provenance, checksum, accessibility, and Chamber behavior pass tests |
| R8 — release | Manifest, documentation, and displayed launchability agree; no candidate is promoted by association |

Failure at any gate is visible and local. A blocked translation does not invalidate an event record, and a strong event record does not make its journey publishable.

## 12. Operating cadence

For each acquisition tranche:

1. select one Tier A initiative and no more than one supporting point cluster;
2. freeze the research question and evidence table;
3. audit sources and rights before drafting an experience;
4. conduct editorial and specialist review;
5. package only cleared excerpts;
6. run coverage, duration, provenance, integrity, accessibility, and Chamber tests;
7. publish a change ledger showing records moved from `none`, remaining blockers, and claims that changed;
8. re-score the backlog against the new live coverage.

The first eight enacted tranches are complete: **Assembly, Republic, Terror** in pilot pack 1.6, **Freedom, Labor, Sovereignty** in pilot pack 1.7, **War, Debt, and the Fiscal State** in pilot pack 1.8, **Revolution and Settlement, 1789–1815** in pilot pack 1.9, **Treaties and the Atlantic Order** in pilot pack 1.10, **Machines, Patents, and Production** in pilot pack 1.11, **War for Independence** in pilot pack 1.12, and **Association, Confederation, Amendment** in pilot pack 1.13. The completion audit retires the overlapping **Law after Revolution** proposal. The next acquisition tranche is **Crowd, Testimony, Publicity**.

## 13. Definition of success

The program succeeds when Atrium becomes better at explanation, not merely larger:

- unlaunchable records decline in coherent clusters;
- war, institutions, diplomacy, political economy, religion, and technology acquire causal depth;
- every experience can distinguish primary evidence from editorial interpretation;
- contested events expose uncertainty without collapsing into relativism;
- runtime text remains rights-cleared, provenance-rich, and non-repetitive;
- the history architecture remains comprehensible without being organized around current political salience.
