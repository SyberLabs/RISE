The Liminal Archive

A curated corpus for symbolic reading practice.
Structured attention. Rigorous numinosity.

Structured not chaotic Transcendent not escapist Crystalline not opaque Generative not finished

Liminal — non-substantial, composed of the space between. The name captures the dual nature: structure and vision, remembrance and futurity, the library and the spaceship.

The Archive is organized by resonance, not genre. Each work is selected for its capacity to induce state, install pattern, generate connection, and reward repeated engagement.

PART TWO: TEXTUAL ARCHITECTURE

Books for the Mind

Tier One: The Foundational Texts

Return to these throughout life.

Italo Calvino — Cosmicomics (1965)

Resonance: ████████████████████ [Maximal]
Mode: Mytho-scientific fabulation
Use: Compression, wonder, chamber content

Old Qfwfq narrates the origin of the universe as personal anecdote. Each story compresses cosmological spans into emotional intimacy. "All at One Point" — all matter concentrated in a single point, and someone makes tagliatelle. This is how to write about scale.

Essential stories: "All at One Point", "A Sign in Space", "The Distance of the Moon"

Paul Klee — Pedagogical Sketchbook (1925)

Resonance: ████████████████████ [Maximal]
Mode: Diagram as philosophy
Use: Visual grammar, Astrotype foundation

Klee's Bauhaus teaching notes. How a point becomes a line becomes a plane becomes a form. Each page is a theorem rendered visually.

Read with: pencil in hand

Vilém Flusser — Into the Universe of Technical Images (1985)

Resonance: ███████████████████░ [Near-maximal]
Mode: Media philosophy as prophecy
Use: Understanding what you're building

Flusser saw it all coming: the image-based society, the apparatus that programs us, the technical imagination required to remain free. Dense, prophetic, urgent. "The Shape of Things" is also essential.

Key concepts: technical image, program, apparatus

Henri Michaux — Miserable Miracle (1956)

Resonance: ███████████████████░ [Near-maximal]
Mode: Psychonautic notation
Use: Understanding your own hypnagogic work

Michaux took mescaline and tried to draw what he saw. The drawings are documents from consciousness exploring itself. The text is the attempt to annotate the unnarratable.

Pair with: his actual drawings

Wassily Kandinsky — Point and Line to Plane (1926)

Resonance: ██████████████████░░ [High]
Mode: Abstract grammar
Use: Foundational theory for visual work

Kandinsky's attempt to establish a science of abstract form. What is a point, really? What forces does a line express? How does the plane contain and direct? Dry in places, but the underlying vision is electric.

Jorge Luis Borges — Ficciones (1944) & Labyrinths (1962)

Resonance: ██████████████████░░ [High]
Mode: Metaphysical fiction
Use: Conceptual expansion, chamber content

Borges constructed labyrinths from ideas. "The Aleph": all of space visible in a single point. "The Library of Babel": all possible books. "Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius": a world invented by a secret society that becomes real. Philosophy as fiction as philosophy.

Essential: "The Aleph", "The Garden of Forking Paths", "Pierre Menard, Author of the Quixote"

Tier Two: Technical Philosophies

These inform the theoretical structure.

Norbert Wiener — Cybernetics (1948)

Resonance: █████████████████░░░ [Strong]
Mode: Systems thinking origin
Use: Theoretical foundation

The founding text. Feedback loops, communication, control. Wiener saw that information was the hidden substrate of all systems. Still generative sixty years later.

Gregory Bateson — Steps to an Ecology of Mind (1972)

Resonance: █████████████████░░░ [Strong]
Mode: Relational epistemology
Use: Understanding mind as pattern

Bateson asked: what is the pattern that connects? Mind is not in the head but in the system — organism plus environment plus their interactions. This is the philosophy of interface consciousness avant la lettre.

Francisco Varela & Humberto Maturana — The Tree of Knowledge (1987)

Resonance: ████████████████░░░░ [Strong]
Mode: Autopoiesis theory
Use: Understanding living systems

What makes something alive? Self-production. Autopoiesis. A cell is a process that produces the components that produce the process. This is the theoretical foundation for treating AI as organism.

Maxwell Maltz — Psycho-Cybernetics (1960)

Resonance: ███████████████░░░░░ [Moderate-Strong]
Mode: Self-image programming
Use: Installation session design

The self-image as servo-mechanism. Visualization as goal-setting. Dated in style, profound in substance. The original consciousness-hacking manual.

Marshall McLuhan — Understanding Media (1964)

Resonance: ███████████████░░░░░ [Moderate-Strong]
Mode: Media theory
Use: Understanding RISE as medium

"The medium is the message." McLuhan understood that technologies reshape us more by their form than their content. RISE is a medium. What does it do to those who use it?

James J. Gibson — The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception (1979)

Resonance: ██████████████░░░░░░ [Moderate]
Mode: Perception theory
Use: Display and interface design

Gibson invented affordance theory: perception is for action, and we perceive possibilities directly. Revolutionary for understanding how humans interact with interfaces.

Resonance: █████████████████░░░ [Strong]

The drawer, the nest, the cellar, the attic. Bachelard asks: what do spaces mean to consciousness?

Mode: Mystical philosophy

Rainer Maria Rilke — Letters to a Young Poet (1929)

Resonance: █████████████░░░░░░░ [Moderate]
Mode: Creative counsel

Octavio Paz — The Bow and the Lyre (1956)

Resonance: █████████████░░░░░░░ [Moderate]
Mode: Poetics as ontology

PART THREE: VISUAL ARCHITECTURE

Films for the Eye

Tier One: The Visions

These are not entertainment. They are transmissions.

Baraka (1992) — Ron Fricke

Resonance: ████████████████████ [Maximal]
State: Planetary consciousness
Use: Expansion, scale recalibration
Duration: 96 minutes

No narrative. No dialogue. Pure image and motion across 24 countries. From temples to factories, from rituals to waste. The human species seen from outside. Essential viewing at least annually.

Pair with: Samsara (2011), Koyaanisqatsi (1982)

Powers of Ten (1977) — Charles & Ray Eames

Resonance: ████████████████████ [Maximal]
State: Scalar vertigo
Use: Understanding scale, RISE intro
Duration: 9 minutes

From a picnic blanket to the edge of the universe. Ten seconds per power of ten. Then inward, to the atom. Nine minutes that permanently alter your sense of scale. Watch repeatedly.

The Quay Brothers — Short Films

Resonance: ███████████████████░ [Near-maximal]
State: Animated uncanny
Use: Object Chamber, symbol work
Duration: Variable (shorts)

Stop-motion animation of impossible spaces. Dust, rust, decay, life. Objects have souls here. "Street of Crocodiles" (1986) is the masterpiece, but explore "Institute Benjamenta" (1995) and "In Absentia" (2000).

Essential: "Street of Crocodiles", "The Comb", "Rehearsals for Extinct Anatomies"

Stalker (1979) — Andrei Tarkovsky

Resonance: ███████████████████░ [Near-maximal]
State: Liminal dread
Use: Extended contemplation
Duration: 163 minutes

Three men enter the Zone, seeking the Room where wishes come true. Nothing happens for long stretches. Everything happens. Tarkovsky films consciousness directly. The Zone is a state of mind.

Also essential: Solaris (1972), Mirror (1975)

Sans Soleil (1983) — Chris Marker

Resonance: ██████████████████░░ [High]
State: Memory-image
Use: Understanding time, memory work
Duration: 100 minutes

An essay film disguised as letters from a traveling cameraman. Images from Japan, Africa, Iceland. The narrator speaks of memory, image, history, loss. The film thinks in images.

Tier Two: The Architectures

Visual environments that teach.

Blade Runner (1982) — Ridley Scott

Resonance: █████████████████░░░ [Strong]
State: Retrofuture noir
Use: Environmental design, atmosphere
Duration: 117 minutes

Los Angeles 2019 as imagined in 1982. The city as character. Rain, neon, decay, density. Still the most influential vision of urban future. Watch the Final Cut.

2001: A Space Odyssey (1968) — Stanley Kubrick

Resonance: █████████████████░░░ [Strong]
State: Cosmic confrontation
Use: Scale, silence, transformation
Duration: 149 minutes

From ape to star child. Kubrick's monolith is an interface with the transcendent. The Star Gate sequence is pure RISE material — watch it isolated as a chamber session.

Playtime (1967) — Jacques Tati

Resonance: ████████████████░░░░ [Strong]
State: Architectural comedy
Use: Understanding space and human scale
Duration: 124 minutes

Tati's Monsieur Hulot wanders through hyper-modern Paris. No close-ups — the frame is always full, demanding active viewing. A meditation on what modernity does to bodies.

Koyaanisqatsi (1982) — Godfrey Reggio

Resonance: █████████████████░░░ [Strong]
State: Civilizational overview
Use: Companion to Baraka
Duration: 86 minutes

"Life out of balance." Time-lapse cities, slowed explosions, Philip Glass score. Less global than Baraka, more focused on technology and speed.

World of Tomorrow (2015) — Don Hertzfeldt

Resonance: ███████████████░░░░░ [Moderate-Strong]
State: Existential animation
Use: Confronting mortality, identity
Duration: 17 minutes

A child receives a visit from her clone from the future. Crude animation carrying profound philosophy. Funny, devastating, true. Watch all three parts.

Fantastic Planet (1973) — René Laloux

Resonance: ██████████████░░░░░░ [Moderate]
State: Alien consciousness
Use: Defamiliarization
Duration: 72 minutes

Humans as pets on an alien world. Czech animation with a deeply strange visual language. Useful for breaking habitual perception.

Rivers and Tides (2001) — Thomas Riedelsheimer

Andy Goldsworthy making impermanent art in nature. Process as content.

Jiro Dreams of Sushi (2011) — David Gelb

Mastery as lifelong practice. 85 years old, still improving.

Cave of Forgotten Dreams (2010) — Werner Herzog

32,000-year-old cave paintings. The first artists.

Leviathan (2012) — Lucien Castaing-Taylor & Véréna Paravel

Industrial fishing as sensory assault. No narration, pure immersion.

Manufactured Landscapes (2006) — Jennifer Baichwal

Edward Burtynsky's photographs of industrial scale. Terrible beauty.

Casey Reas — Process Compendium

Co-creator of Processing. Reas builds visual systems from simple rules. The drawings emerge from process, not intention. Useful for your own generative explorations.

PART FIVE: VISUAL ART

Images for Installation

Tier One: The Systems

Artists who work with systematic processes.

Hilma af Klint — The Paintings for the Temple (1906-1915)

Resonance: ████████████████████ [Maximal]
Mode: Spiritual abstraction
Use: Symbol work, installation sessions

Before Kandinsky, before Mondrian, af Klint was painting abstract cosmologies received through séance. The "Ten Largest" are consciousness maps. Recently rediscovered, essential for understanding art as spiritual practice.

Paul Klee — Complete Works

Resonance: ████████████████████ [Maximal]
Mode: Pedagogical abstraction
Use: Astrotype inspiration, visual grammar

9,000 works. Each one a theorem. Klee taught that art is not representation but the creation of visual equivalents for natural processes. Your practice descends from his.

Key periods: Bauhaus years, Tunisia watercolors, late "angel" works

Cy Twombly — Fifty Days at Iliam (1978)

Resonance: ███████████████████░ [Near-maximal]
Mode: Gestural notation
Use: Understanding mark-making

Ten paintings narrating the Trojan War through scrawl, number, color, name. Twombly writes rather than paints. The line between drawing and writing dissolves.

Agnes Martin — Late Grid Paintings

Resonance: ██████████████████░░ [High]
Mode: Meditative geometry
Use: Contemplation, clearing

Grids so subtle they approach invisibility. Martin said her work was about "perfection as we are aware of it in our minds." Hours can disappear into one painting.

Mark Rothko — Chapel Paintings (1971)

Resonance: ██████████████████░░ [High]
Mode: Color field transcendence
Use: State induction, installation content

The final paintings, installed in a nondenominational chapel in Houston. Purple-black fields that absorb. Rothko said he painted tragedy, ecstasy, doom. The chapel proves it.

Henri Michaux — Mescaline Drawings

Resonance: ███████████████████░ [Near-maximal]
Mode: Psychonautic notation
Use: Direct parallel to Astrotypes

Michaux's attempts to document chemically-altered perception. The drawings are seismographs of consciousness. Essential companion to your own hypnagogic work.

John Cage — Ryoanji Drawings (1983-1992)

Resonance: ████████████████░░░░ [Strong]
Mode: Chance operations
Use: Understanding systematic drawing

Cage traced around stones using I Ching-determined positions. The system generates the form. Drawing as performed process.

Sol LeWitt — Wall Drawings

Resonance: ███████████████░░░░░ [Moderate-Strong]
Mode: Instruction-based art
Use: Understanding conceptual drawing

LeWitt wrote instructions; others executed them. The work is the idea. "The idea becomes a machine that makes the art." Relevant for RISE session design.

Emma Kunz — Healing Drawings

Resonance: ██████████████░░░░░░ [Moderate]
Mode: Radiesthetic art

Swiss healer who created geometric drawings through pendulum dowsing. She said they were not art but research. Discovered posthumously, now recognized as visionary.

The Archive as Living System

The Oracular Archive is not static.

It grows through:

  1. Encounter — You engage with works, discover new ones
  2. Annotation — You note what resonates, what functions
  3. Connection — You find rhymes between works
  4. Integration — Works enter your own production
  5. Recursion — Your production enters the archive

The archive is a memory palace.

The works are rooms.

You walk through them.

They walk through you.
